International Relations Theory

Realism Liberalism Constructivism Critical / Marxist English School & Feminist IR Comparison

International Relations (IR) theory offers competing lenses for explaining why states and other actors behave as they do in world politics — why wars break out, why states cooperate, and what forces (power, institutions, ideas, or class) really drive the international system. The five families below — Realism, Liberalism, Constructivism, Critical & Marxist theory, and the English School / Feminist IR — remain the core paradigms taught in most IR curricula.

Power & Anarchy

Realism

Realism holds that the international system is anarchic — there is no higher authority above sovereign states — so states must rely on self-help to survive. States are the primary actors, are assumed to behave rationally, and pursue power and security as core interests. Cooperation is possible but always constrained by the underlying struggle for relative power.

Classical Realism

Grounds the drive for power in human nature and the flawed, self-interested character of political actors. States pursue power because leaders and peoples inherently do.

Hans Morgenthau — Politics Among Nations (1948), the founding text of post-war realism, laid out six principles of political realism centered on "interest defined as power."
E.H. Carr — The Twenty Years' Crisis, 1919–1939 (1939), a critique of interwar liberal idealism.
Thucydides — History of the Peloponnesian War (c. 5th century BCE), the "Melian Dialogue" is a touchstone realist text ("the strong do what they can and the weak suffer what they must").

Neorealism (Structural Realism)

Locates the cause of state behavior not in human nature but in the structure of the international system — its anarchic ordering principle and the distribution of capabilities (polarity) among states.

Kenneth Waltz — Theory of International Politics (1979), the foundational neorealist text; also Man, the State, and War (1959).
John Mearsheimer — The Tragedy of Great Power Politics (2001), developed offensive realism: great powers maximize power because security is never guaranteed.
Stephen Walt — The Origins of Alliances (1987), proposed "balance of threat" over pure balance-of-power logic.

Key concepts & debates
  • Balance of power — states form alliances to prevent any one power from dominating the system.
  • Security dilemma (John Herz, 1950) — one state's efforts to increase its security (e.g., arms buildup) make others less secure, spiraling into arms races.
  • Offensive vs. defensive realism — Mearsheimer argues states should maximize power; Waltz and defensive realists (e.g., Charles Glaser) argue states seek only an appropriate amount of power to survive.
  • Hegemonic stability theory (Robert Gilpin, War and Change in World Politics, 1981) — a dominant hegemon can stabilize the international order.
Institutions & Cooperation

Liberalism

Liberal theory shares realism's assumption of anarchy but argues that cooperation is not merely possible but common, sustained through international institutions, economic interdependence, and shared democratic norms. States are not the only relevant actors — international organizations, NGOs, and domestic political processes also shape outcomes.

Neoliberal Institutionalism

Robert Keohane — After Hegemony (1984), argues institutions reduce transaction costs and enable cooperation even under anarchy.
Keohane & Nye — Power and Interdependence (1977), coined "complex interdependence" as an alternative to realist state-centrism.

Democratic Peace Theory

Michael Doyle — "Kant, Liberal Legacies, and Foreign Affairs" (1983), revived the empirical claim that established democracies rarely if ever go to war with one another.
Bruce Russett — Grasping the Democratic Peace (1993).

Liberal Intergovernmentalism

Andrew Moravcsik — "Taking Preferences Seriously: A Liberal Theory of International Politics" (International Organization, 1997), grounds state preferences in domestic society and economic interests rather than systemic structure.

Roots & key concepts
  • Immanuel Kant — Perpetual Peace (1795), the philosophical foundation for liberal IR, proposing that republican constitutions, a pacific federation of states, and commerce would produce lasting peace.
  • Woodrow Wilson — early 20th-century "idealism," championed collective security and the League of Nations, the precursor to institutionalist liberalism.
  • Absolute vs. relative gains — liberals argue states care about absolute gains from cooperation, not merely gains relative to rivals (contra realism).
  • Regime theory — Stephen Krasner (ed.), International Regimes (1983), on how norms and rules structure issue-areas like trade or the environment.
Ideas, Norms & Identity

Constructivism

Constructivism rejects the materialism of realism and liberalism, arguing that international structures are made of shared ideas, norms, and identities rather than fixed material facts. Anarchy itself has no inherent meaning — as Alexander Wendt famously put it, "anarchy is what states make of it." Interests are not given but socially constructed through interaction.

Foundational works

Alexander Wendt — "Anarchy is What States Make of It" (International Organization, 1992) and Social Theory of International Politics (1999), the canonical statement of constructivist IR.
Nicholas Onuf — World of Our Making (1989), coined the term "constructivism" in IR.

Norms & identity research

Martha Finnemore & Kathryn Sikkink — "International Norm Dynamics and Political Change" (1998), the "norm life cycle" model (emergence → cascade → internalization).
Peter Katzenstein (ed.) — The Culture of National Security (1996), on how identity and norms shape security policy.

Constructivism has since branched into conventional (positivist, e.g., Finnemore, Katzenstein) and critical/post-structuralist strands, and strongly influences the study of human rights norms, humanitarian intervention, and the diffusion of institutional models across states.

Class, Power & Emancipation

Critical & Marxist Theories

Critical theories reject the problem-solving stance of realism and liberalism — which take the existing world order for granted — in favor of interrogating whose interests that order serves. Marxist-inflected IR traces global politics to capitalist class relations and economic structures rather than state-level power politics.

World-Systems & Dependency Theory

Immanuel Wallerstein — The Modern World-System (1974–89, 4 vols.), divides the globe into core, periphery, and semi-periphery zones bound by unequal exchange.
Raúl Prebisch / Andre Gunder Frank — dependency theory, arguing peripheral "underdevelopment" is produced by, not separate from, core capitalist development.

Critical Theory & Gramscian IR

Robert Cox — "Social Forces, States and World Orders: Beyond International Relations Theory" (1981); famous distinction between problem-solving theory and critical theory. Applied Gramsci's concept of hegemony to explain how a dominant class's worldview becomes globally naturalized.
Antonio Gramsci — Prison Notebooks (written 1929–35), the concept of cultural/ideological hegemony that underpins neo-Gramscian IR.

Related strands
  • Frankfurt School critical theory — Jürgen Habermas's emancipatory epistemology influenced Cox and Andrew Linklater's cosmopolitan critical IR (The Transformation of Political Community, 1998).
  • Post-colonial IR — critiques Eurocentrism in mainstream theory; e.g., Sankaran Krishna, Siba Grovogui.
  • Post-structuralist IR — R.B.J. Walker, Inside/Outside: International Relations as Political Theory (1993), questions the inside/outside, sovereign/anarchic binary itself.
Society, Order & Gender

English School & Feminist IR

English School (International Society)

Argues states form an "international society" bound by shared rules, institutions, and diplomatic culture — occupying a middle ground ("via media") between realism's anarchy and liberalism's cosmopolitanism.

Hedley Bull — The Anarchical Society (1977), the school's central text, distinguishing "system," "society," and "world society."
Martin Wight — International Theory: The Three Traditions (published 1991), mapped Realist, Rationalist (Grotian), and Revolutionist traditions.

Feminist IR

Examines how gender shapes and is shaped by international politics — critiquing the masculinized assumptions (autonomy, rationality, power-as-domination) embedded in mainstream theory.

Cynthia Enloe — Bananas, Beaches and Bases (1989), showed how militarism and global political economy depend on gendered labor.
J. Ann Tickner — Gender in International Relations (1992), reformulated Morgenthau's realist principles from a feminist standpoint.

How they talk past — and to — each other

  • Realism and liberalism are both broadly rationalist / positivist — they share materialist assumptions and largely disagree about whether the material world favors conflict or cooperation. This is why the 1990s "neo-neo debate" (neorealism vs. neoliberal institutionalism) could be framed as a family quarrel over a shared methodology.
  • Constructivism is often described as a bridge between rationalist theories and more radical post-positivist approaches: it uses conventional social-science methods but insists that the material world realists and liberals treat as fixed is itself given meaning by ideas.
  • Critical and Marxist theories reject the entire rationalist/problem-solving frame, per Cox's line that "theory is always for someone and for some purpose" — the sharpest methodological break in the field.
  • The English School and feminist IR are often read as correctives that reintroduce, respectively, shared normative order and gender as a category of analysis into debates the "big three" (realism, liberalism, constructivism) tend to overlook.
