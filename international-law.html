International Law

Sources Treaties & Statehood Human Rights Humanitarian & Criminal Law Law of the Sea & Economy Institutions & Comparison

International law is the body of rules and principles that govern relations between sovereign states and, increasingly, international organizations, corporations, and individuals. Unlike domestic law, it has no single legislature, executive, or compulsory court — its authority rests on state consent, custom, and institutional practice. The regimes below — sources & foundations, treaties & statehood, human rights law, humanitarian & criminal law, and law of the sea & international economic law — form the core structure taught in most public international law curricula.

Foundations

Sources of International Law

Article 38(1) of the Statute of the International Court of Justice (1945) is the standard reference for the sources of international law: international conventions (treaties), international custom, general principles of law recognized by civilized nations, and — as subsidiary means — judicial decisions and the teachings of highly qualified publicists.

Treaties (Conventional Law)

Binding written agreements between states, governed by the Vienna Convention on the Law of Treaties (VCLT, 1969) — often called the "treaty on treaties." Binds only consenting parties (pacta tertiis nec nocent nec prosunt) unless a rule also reflects custom.

Customary International Law

Arises from two elements: state practice (general and consistent conduct) and opinio juris (a belief that the practice is legally required). Binds all states except "persistent objectors." Established in the North Sea Continental Shelf cases (ICJ, 1969).

Key concepts & hierarchy
  • Jus cogens (peremptory norms) — non-derogable rules (e.g., prohibitions on genocide, slavery, torture, aggression) that override any conflicting treaty (VCLT Art. 53).
  • Erga omnes obligations — duties owed to the international community as a whole, not just to a single injured state (Barcelona Traction, ICJ, 1970).
  • Soft law — non-binding instruments (declarations, resolutions, guidelines) that shape expectations and can crystallize into custom over time, e.g., the Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948).
  • Monism vs. dualism — competing theories of how international law enters domestic legal systems: automatically (monist) or only through domestic incorporation (dualist).
Statehood, Sovereignty & Responsibility

Subjects, Treaties & State Responsibility

States are the primary "subjects" of international law, but international organizations (e.g., the UN), and to a limited degree individuals and corporations, also hold rights and duties under it.

Statehood

The Montevideo Convention (1933) criteria: a permanent population, a defined territory, an effective government, and the capacity to enter into relations with other states. Recognition by other states is politically significant but, under the declaratory theory, not legally constitutive of statehood.

Law of Treaties (VCLT)

Governs formation, interpretation (pacta sunt servanda — agreements must be kept), reservations, invalidity (e.g., coercion, error), and termination (including rebus sic stantibus — fundamental change of circumstances).

State Responsibility

The ILC's Articles on State Responsibility (2001) set out when an internationally wrongful act (attribution + breach) triggers a state's obligation to cease the conduct and make reparation — restitution, compensation, or satisfaction.

Sovereignty, jurisdiction & the UN Charter regime
  • Sovereign equality — UN Charter Art. 2(1); each state, regardless of size or power, possesses equal legal standing.
  • Non-intervention & the use of force — Art. 2(4) prohibits the threat or use of force against territorial integrity or political independence, subject to Chapter VII Security Council authorization (Art. 42) and self-defense (Art. 51).
  • Jurisdiction — territorial, nationality, protective, passive personality, and (contested) universal jurisdiction bases for a state to prescribe and enforce law.
  • State immunity — the restrictive doctrine distinguishes sovereign acts (acta jure imperii, immune) from commercial acts (acta jure gestionis, not immune).
Individual Rights & Protection

International Human Rights Law

Human rights law binds states to protect individuals — including, in principle, against their own government — marking a major departure from the classical state-centric model. Its modern architecture began with the Universal Declaration of Human Rights (UDHR, 1948) and expanded into binding treaty law.

The International Bill of Rights

The UDHR plus the two 1966 covenants: the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights (ICCPR) and the International Covenant on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights (ICESCR) — reflecting a Cold War-era split between "negative" liberty rights and "positive" welfare rights.

Specialized & regional regimes

Topic-specific treaties (CEDAW 1979, CRC 1989, CAT 1984, CRPD 2006) and regional courts — the European Court of Human Rights, Inter-American Court of Human Rights, and African Court on Human and Peoples' Rights — which offer individuals direct routes to binding adjudication unavailable at the UN level.

Enforcement & recurring debates
  • Treaty bodies — UN committees (e.g., the Human Rights Committee) monitor compliance via state reports and, where accepted, individual complaints — but lack direct enforcement power.
  • Universality vs. cultural relativism — a longstanding debate over whether human rights norms are genuinely universal or reflect particular (often Western liberal) traditions.
  • Responsibility to Protect (R2P) — a 2005 World Summit doctrine holding that states have a duty to protect populations from genocide, war crimes, ethnic cleansing, and crimes against humanity, with the international community stepping in if a state fails.
  • Derogation — most civil-political rights (not jus cogens rights like the prohibition on torture) may be suspended "in time of public emergency" under ICCPR Art. 4.
Conflict, Accountability & Atrocity

Humanitarian & Criminal Law

Two closely related but distinct regimes: international humanitarian law (IHL), or the law of armed conflict, which regulates how wars are fought; and international criminal law (ICL), which holds individuals personally accountable for the gravest violations.

International Humanitarian Law

Codified chiefly by the four 1949 Geneva Conventions and their 1977 Additional Protocols, plus customary rules. Governed by core principles: distinction (combatants vs. civilians), proportionality, military necessity, and precaution. Applies alongside — but distinct from — the jus ad bellum (law on when force may be used).

International Criminal Law

The Rome Statute (1998) established the permanent International Criminal Court (ICC, seated in The Hague), with jurisdiction over four core crimes: genocide, crimes against humanity, war crimes, and (since 2018) the crime of aggression. Built on the precedent of the Nuremberg and Tokyo Tribunals (1945–46) and the ad hoc ICTY/ICTR of the 1990s.

Key concepts & institutional limits
  • Complementarity — the ICC only acts when national courts are "unwilling or unable" to genuinely prosecute (Rome Statute Art. 17); it is a court of last resort, not a substitute for domestic justice.
  • Command responsibility — commanders can be held liable for crimes committed by subordinates they knew of, or should have known of, and failed to prevent or punish.
  • Universal jurisdiction — some grave crimes (piracy, genocide, torture) may be prosecuted by any state regardless of where they occurred or the nationality of the accused.
  • Jurisdictional gaps — the ICC has no independent enforcement arm and relies on state cooperation for arrests; major powers including the US, China, and Russia are not parties to the Rome Statute.
Territory, Resources & Trade

Law of the Sea & International Economic Law

Law of the Sea (UNCLOS)

The UN Convention on the Law of the Sea (1982), often called a "constitution for the oceans," establishes maritime zones radiating from a state's baseline: territorial sea (12 nm, full sovereignty), contiguous zone (24 nm), exclusive economic zone (EEZ, 200 nm, resource rights), and the continental shelf. Beyond national jurisdiction lies the "high seas" and the deep seabed, designated the "common heritage of mankind."

International Economic Law

Governs cross-border trade and investment through institutions like the World Trade Organization (WTO, 1995), whose Dispute Settlement Body issues binding rulings on trade disputes, and a dense web of bilateral investment treaties (BITs) providing for investor-state dispute settlement (ISDS).

Also within this cluster: international environmental law (e.g., the Paris Agreement, 2015; Convention on Biological Diversity, 1992), built on principles like common but differentiated responsibilities and the precautionary principle, which increasingly intersects with both the law of the sea (ocean acidification, fisheries) and economic law (carbon border measures).

The recurring tension: consent vs. authority

  • International law's core structural feature is that it binds primarily through state consent — a state is generally not bound by a treaty it hasn't ratified, and the ICJ has jurisdiction over a state only if that state has accepted it. This makes the system fundamentally different from a domestic legal order with compulsory jurisdiction.
  • Jus cogens norms and the UN Security Council's Chapter VII powers are the two main exceptions to pure consent-based obligation — the former binds regardless of consent, the latter can bind even non-consenting states via Council resolution.
  • Enforcement remains the field's persistent weak point: most compliance is driven by reciprocity, reputational cost, and domestic incorporation rather than centralized coercion — which is why international law is sometimes called "law without a sovereign."
  • The proliferation of specialized regimes (trade, human rights, environment, criminal law) since 1990 has raised concerns about the "fragmentation" of international law into self-contained regimes with inconsistent rules and overlapping tribunals.
